Davide Di Giorgio — Good Enough

Find where to start.

The cost of good enough you just calculated isn't random. It's coming from one of three places, and only one of them is really yours to start with.

Over 30+ years I've worked across industries most people never combine, from theatre, hospitality, and education to leading a billion-dollar brand's expansion across two countries. The same gap shows up in every one of them, and it almost always traces to one of three things: your standards, your team's beliefs, or the conditions around them. Just three.

They work together, so we take them one at a time, in order, and stop at the first one that's actually yours. A few minutes, start to finish.

One condition for this to work: be completely objective with yourself. Not the version you'd give in a meeting, but what's actually true when no one is looking. This is only as sharp as the objectivity you bring to it.

And it's going to feel almost too simple. The tools you've been handed to fix this are usually complicated; that's how they justify themselves. The simplicity here isn't a shortcut. It's the whole point. It's what's left once you strip away everything that doesn't work.

Standards

Start here, because almost every gap gets blamed on everything but this.

Ask yourself

Would at least three different people, from someone junior to someone senior, describe what "excellent" looks like in their role the same way?

What a clear standard actually looks like:

Hospitality"Great service" is a preference. "A guest is greeted within ten seconds, by name where we know it" is a standard.
Office"Good analysis" is a preference. "Every report opens with the decision it's recommending" is a standard.
Multi-unit"Keep it clean" is a preference. "The ten-point close every location runs, the same way, every night" is a standard.

The trap: "They know it, it's written down, we've trained on it." Written, recited, reviewed is a document that's been presented. It isn't a standard people could reproduce with the page taken away. If they couldn't, you have a poster, not a standard. Matching, specific answers means the bar is real; vague or conflicting ones ("good work," "hustle") mean it's a preference in disguise.

Standards · Go find out

Then don't guess. Go and find out this week. Ask at least three different people, separately, from someone junior to someone senior.

Ask them, word for word

"Describe what excellent looks like in your role."

Then say nothing. Let the silence do the work.

The only things you add: "Tell me more about that" when they land on something concrete, or "What else?" when they finish a thought. The first answer is rarely the real one; the truer answer surfaces a beat later, so give it room.

Listen for whether they describe the same thing, concretely: not "good work," but the actual behaviours. When you have your answer, come back and pick up here:

Beliefs

The bar's clear, and they can do the work. The next question to ask is whether they do it when no one is watching.

(One gut-check first: if some genuinely couldn't do this even with your help, that's a skill gap, not belief. A training conversation, and a different fix.)

Ask yourself

When someone reaches beyond good enough and it costs them, in a moment no one would ever catch them skipping, what happens to that person on your team?

The kind of moment I mean:

The catchThey spot a mistake no one else would ever have caught. Fixing it takes real effort.Fix it, or let it ship?
The honest flagThey realise they got something wrong that no one else noticed.Own it, or let it slide?
The shortcutA step they could skip, with no one watching and nothing breaking today.Do it right, or take the shortcut?
The detailA finish is "good enough" but not quite right, and no one will ever inspect it.Make it right, or move on?
The handoffThey're leaving something for the next person. No one tracks the state they leave it in.Set it up well, or just enough?
The easy versionA customer would never know the difference either way.Give them the better version, or the easy one?

Here's what actually tells the story

When they do reach beyond good enough, when they choose the harder right that no one asked for, what happens next? Are they seen? Celebrated? Or does it pass unnoticed and cost them? Their honest read of your response is what decides whether their best ever shows up again.

Start here · Standards

This is a standards problem.

If the answers don't line up, the bar isn't set. It's a preference no one else can see, let alone hit. And nothing below this is even readable until it's clear.

Your next most powerful step

Here's the part most leaders get wrong: they write a cleaner version and hand it down. Do that and it lands as one more thing imposed on people, and an imposed standard buys you compliance at best.

If the standard genuinely isn't clear, build it with the people who live it, in their own words, so it's theirs. Then install it over time and reinforce it in the moment, in real time, not in a memo. A standard people helped shape, and hear again when it matters, is one they'll hold when you're not there.

One warning worth its weight: most leaders install a standard by dropping the hammer, with new rules, monitoring, and consequences. That's correction, and it usually widens the gap instead of closing it. Setting a standard people own is a different skill. And if you're sure you've already mastered it because you've done it before, here's the honest question the number you just saw is asking: then why is it that high?

Your starting point · Beliefs

This is a belief problem.

The bar's clear, they can do it, but the reach is gone. Once people learn that good enough and their best land in the same place, they'll give you good enough and keep the rest. You can't announce your way out of that.

You saw it in those moments: the mistake caught, the shortcut refused, the handoff done right. None of it shows on a dashboard.

Your next most powerful step

This week, pick one person and one thing that could genuinely be theirs. Then make it a real ask, not a hand-off. Say:

"I'd like this to be yours to own."

Then stop, and wait for them to actually accept it. This is an invitation, not an assignment. Once they've said yes:

"I'm not going to hover. I trust you with it. I'm here if you want me, genuinely, to think it through together, never to check up on you. It's yours."

Then give them the room. What the work becomes when it's truly theirs, and they know you're for them, is your reading of belief.

And find one thing that limits or punishes reaching. It might be a correction that could have been a coaching moment. Just as often it's a gate, a sign-off, a permission step, or a process that adds friction to doing more than the minimum. Whatever it is, take it away.

The good-news answer · Conditions

Your people are reaching. Now protect it.

The belief is there. So the question that matters now is why.

Ask yourself

Why are my people reaching beyond good enough? What, specifically, is making them do it?

If your honest answer is "I have great people," that's not an answer. It's luck. And luck doesn't transfer, promote, or scale. It leaves the day that person does, and takes the unnamed system with it.

Luck vs a mechanism you could hand to another team:

Developing peopleLuck"We hire well."A mechanism"Every new hire shadows our best for two weeks, then keeps a mentor to go to through their first 90 days."
Improving the workLuck"Our team just cares."A mechanism"Every project ends with a fifteen-minute what-worked, and those lessons are built into how the next one starts."
Across sitesLuck"That site's just strong."A mechanism"Every location runs the same five-minute pre-shift huddle, every shift."

Your next most powerful step

Say the answer out loud, in one sentence, the way you'd hand it to a brand-new manager. If you can't finish the sentence, that's the work: make the invisible thing sayable, then repeatable. Give it to one other team and watch whether it travels. If it does, you have a system. If it doesn't, you've found the next thing to build.
